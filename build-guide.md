**Tools Required:** Shovel, Wheelbarrow, Drill Mixer, Level, Tape Measure, Plastic Sheeting

---

## 📋 Project Overview
This guide details the construction of the **OpenCell Thermal-Mechanical Energy System**, a zero-electric cooling and power generation unit. The system consists of three integrated loops:
1.  **Solar Thermal Collector:** Vertical panels of OpenCell Aerocement.
2.  **Subterranean Cold Loop:** An underground labyrinth for passive cooling.
3.  **Mechanical Power Train:** Stirling/Rocket Mass engine integration.

**Estimated Build Time:** 2–3 Weeks (depending on curing times)  
**Cost Estimate:** $400–$800 (excluding land excavation equipment)

---

## 🏗️ Phase 1: Site Preparation & Excavation

### 1.1 Location Selection
- **Sun Exposure:** Choose a south-facing slope (Northern Hemisphere) with no shade from trees or buildings between 9 AM and 4 PM.
- **Soil Type:** Clay-loam is ideal for holding the trench shape. Sandy soil requires additional bracing.
- **Distance:** Locate the cold loop at least 10 feet away from the house foundation to prevent structural settling issues.

### 1.2 Digging the Cold Loop Trench
The "Cold Capture" loop relies on the earth's constant 55°F temperature at depth.

1.  **Dimensions:** Dig a trench **10 feet deep**, **4 feet wide**, and **20 feet long**.
    - *Note:* Depth is critical. Shallower trenches will fluctuate with surface temps.
2.  **Shape:** Create a "U" or "L" shaped labyrinth inside the trench to maximize air path length.
    - **Path Length:** Aim for at least 40 feet of total air channel length.
3.  **Walls:** Smooth the walls as much as possible. If soil is loose, install temporary wooden bracing or line with heavy-duty plastic to prevent collapse.

## 🧱 Phase 2: Constructing the Aerocement Panels

### 2.1 Formwork Construction
You will need to cast vertical panels (5 ft wide x 13 ft tall).

1.  **Materials:** 2x4 lumber, plywood sheets, screws.
2.  **Assembly:** Build a rectangular frame (5' x 13') with internal cross-bracing every 2 feet to prevent bowing under wet cement weight.
3.  **Release Agent:** Coat the inside of the mold with cooking oil or commercial release agent.

### 2.2 Mixing the Aerocement
*Refer to `aerocement-formula.md` for the exact recipe.*

1.  **Batch Size:** Mix in batches of 1 cubic foot to ensure proper aeration.
2.  **The "Fluff" Test:** Stir until the mixture visibly expands and holds microscopic bubbles.
3.  **Pouring:** Pour into the vertical mold. Tap the sides of the mold gently with a rubber mallet to settle the mix and remove large voids (do not vibrate aggressively, or you will collapse the open cells).
4.  **Reinforcement:** Insert a lightweight wire mesh or bamboo rebar for tensile strength before the final layer of cement sets.

### 2.3 Curing
1.  **Cover:** Immediately cover the wet panels with plastic sheeting to retain moisture.
2.  **Wait:** Leave in the mold for **48 hours**.
3.  **Demold:** Carefully remove the wood.
4.  **Final Cure:** Mist with water twice daily for **7 days**. Do not expose to direct sun during the first 3 days to prevent cracking.

## 🔗 Phase 3: Installing the Cold Loop

### 3.1 Lining the Trench
1.  **Base Layer:** Lay a 6-inch layer of crushed gravel at the bottom of the trench for drainage.
2.  **Aerocement Lining:** Place the cured Aerocement blocks along the walls and floor of the trench labyrinth.
    - *Goal:* Create a continuous, porous surface for air contact.
    - *Sealing:* Use a thin layer of pure cement slurry (no bubbles) to seal joints between blocks, ensuring air only flows through the designed channels.

### 3.2 Air Intake & Exhaust
1.  **Intake Pipe:** Install a 6-inch diameter PVC or metal pipe leading from the surface into the top of the trench labyrinth.
    - *Filter:* Install a mesh screen at the intake to prevent debris entry.
2.  **Exhaust Pipe:** Install a second pipe at the lowest point of the labyrinth to collect cooled air.
3.  **Fan (Optional):** A small, solar-powered DC fan can be installed at the intake to force air through the system during low-wind periods.

---

## ⚙️ Phase 4: Mechanical Power Integration

### 4.1 Engine Placement
- **Hot Side:** Mount the Stirling engine or Rocket Mass heater adjacent to the Aerocement collector panels.
- **Cold Side:** Connect the engine's cold exhaust directly to the **Exhaust Pipe** of the subterranean loop.
    - *Critical:* The temperature differential (ΔT) drives the engine. Maximize contact between the cold pipe and the 55°F earth.

### 4.2 Transmission Setup
1.  **Flywheel:** Attach a heavy flywheel (e.g., old car wheel or concrete disc) to the engine crankshaft.
2.  **Belt Drive:** Run a V-belt from the flywheel to the load (water pump, grain mill, saw).
    - *Safety:* Install a belt guard to prevent entanglement.
3.  **Alternator (Optional):** Connect an alternator via a clutch mechanism that only engages when RPM exceeds 1500.

---

## 🧪 Phase 5: Testing & Validation

### 5.1 The "Cold" Test
1.  Turn on the intake fan (or wait for wind).
2.  Measure air temperature at the **Intake** (ambient).
3.  Measure air temperature at the **Exhaust**.
    - **Target:** Exhaust temp should be **35°F–45°F** lower than intake on hot days.
4.  Check for condensation: You should see water dripping from the exhaust pipe. Collect this for drinking or irrigation.

### 5.2 The "Power" Test
1.  Heat the Hot Side of the engine using solar concentration or biomass.
2.  Observe flywheel rotation.
3.  Engage a mechanical load (e.g., lift a 10lb weight).
    - **Success Criteria:** The system should sustain rotation under load without stalling.

## ⚠️ Safety Warnings
- **Excavation:** Never enter a trench deeper than 4 feet without shoring. Collapse is fatal.
- **Cement:** Wear gloves and eye protection. Cement causes chemical burns.
- **Carbon Monoxide:** If using a Rocket Mass heater, ensure proper venting. Never operate indoors without ventilation.
- **Mechanical Parts:** Keep hair and loose clothing away from rotating belts and flywheels.
